# Objection Playbook — REV Sales Engineer Demos + Objection Coaching

**Purpose:** Give reps exact response scripts for the 30-minute Sprint discovery/demo context.

Each objection response follows: **Acknowledge → Reframe → Evidence → Close attempt**.

## Usage
- Pause for the objection fully. Do not interrupt.
- Restate their concern in their words.
- Use exactly one script, then ask a close-aligned question.

---

## 1) Objection: "too expensive"
**Acknowledge:**
"I hear you — price sensitivity is smart in this environment."

**Reframe:**
"Before we discuss price, I want to make sure we’re comparing this to the cost of delay. Right now your team is paying for rework, trust risk, and unplanned recovery time. The Sprint replaces broad guesswork with a ranked plan in 5 days."

**Evidence:**
"The Sprint is fixed at $5,000 and includes four client-ready outputs: scoring, prioritized gap list, 30/60/90 roadmap, and executive decision memo. Most objections at this stage are solved by reducing unknowns—not by discounting."

**Close attempt:**
"Would a [payment split 50/50 on sign and delivery] make this easier while preserving scope and timeline?"

---

## 2) Objection: "not the right time"
**Acknowledge:**
"Absolutely fair — timing is often the deciding factor."

**Reframe:**
"When teams defer the issue, it usually gets addressed in firefighting mode and costs more later. The Sprint is specifically to compress and clarify, not expand project scope."

**Evidence:**
"It creates a single 5-day decision input: what’s breaking most, what to fix first, and who owns each action. That usually reduces urgency fatigue versus a long, fragmented assessment."

**Close attempt:**
"Is the timing issue availability, budget cycle, or priority competition? If it’s priority competition, can we start a focused scope this week and defer broader rollout to after that?"

---

## 3) Objection: "we do this internally"
**Acknowledge:**
"That’s a strong capability and I respect teams that run well internally."

**Reframe:**
"In that case this should feel like an independent quality layer, not a replacement: we bring structure, benchmarks, and evidence packaging your team can use internally."

**Evidence:**
"The scoring output is designed to be handoff-ready, with owners, artifact evidence, confidence levels, and a roadmap your internal team can execute directly."

**Close attempt:**
"Could we treat this as a dual-track diagnostic where your team leads execution and we provide a measurable scoring + governance baseline in 5 days?"

---

## 4) Objection: "we need to think about it"
**Acknowledge:**
"Totally reasonable. Important purchases deserve thoughtfulness."

**Reframe:**
"Let me help by narrowing what exactly needs thought. Is it fit, risk, timing, or commercial terms?"

**Evidence:**
"Most delays happen when objections are bundled. We can usually resolve them in one pass by mapping each to a specific item: scope, owners, timeline, proof needs, and cost."

**Close attempt:**
"If I can answer the exact point of uncertainty now, can you give me a clear go/no-go by [date]?"

---

## 5) Objection: "send me something to review"
**Acknowledge:**
"That’s a good control and I can do that."

**Reframe:**
"I don’t want you to review a generic deck that doesn’t match your specifics. The fastest path is sending a one-pager tied to your situation and your KPIs."

**Evidence:**
"I can send a custom one-page brief with: problem sentence, target outcome, scope, timeline, cost, and decision criteria. It’s usually more useful than a long proposal for internal prep."

**Close attempt:**
"Great—if I send it now, can you share it with [DM name] before we close the loop at [date]?"

---

## 6) Objection: "who else have you done this for?"
**Acknowledge:**
"Good question—credibility is important."

**Reframe:**
"Relevance is better than name-dropping. I can share the type of teams and problems we support, including outcomes, instead of just a client list."

**Evidence:**
"The framework is built around measurable outcomes, not vertical branding. Typical clients use it when AI output quality or agent reliability is affecting operational cost, customer trust, or release quality."

**Close attempt:**
"Would it help if I send two anonymized examples and a reference structure on scope/metrics before your internal review?"

---

## 7) Objection: "what's the ROI?"
**Acknowledge:**
"Great to focus on ROI — that should be a first-class criterion."

**Reframe:**
"For this phase, the cleanest ROI is decision quality + cycle reduction, not only immediate financial uplift. The Sprint gives you the highest-confidence next actions so you stop spending on low-value work."

**Evidence:**
"You get a ranked intervention list tied to cost-of-delay and risk, plus a plan with owners and sequencing. That shortens time to first funded action and reduces ambiguity in leadership decisions."

**Close attempt:**
"Can we treat ROI here as ‘faster certainty + less wrong-work for 30/60/90’ and review economic impact after roadmap execution?"

---

## 8) Objection: "we have compliance concerns"
**Acknowledge:**
"Completely valid — especially if you’re handling regulated, customer-facing, or safety-critical outputs."

**Reframe:**
"That makes AMC a better fit, because the Sprint is specifically about evidence-backed controls and auditable readiness, not a blind experimentation phase."

**Evidence:**
"We score governance, controls, verification, and evidence confidence, and every finding links to specific artifacts and owners. That gives you a traceable baseline for legal, security, and QA review."

**Close attempt:**
"Would you prefer that I walk the roadmap with your compliance or legal-facing owner in the second half of the kickoff so their concerns are built into priorities?"

---

## 9) Objection: "We’re comparing vendors"
**Acknowledge:**
"Of course—smart teams compare rigor, not just price."

**Reframe:**
"Use a practical test: whichever partner can turn data into decision-ready priorities quickest and with least ambiguity is usually safer."

**Evidence:**
"Our deliverable set is fixed and auditable: scorecard, evidence confidence, ranked gap list, and 30/60/90 roadmap. That structure is explicit and testable in week one."

**Close attempt:**
"Would it help if I map our approach directly to your three evaluation criteria before your decision deadline?"

---

## 10) Objection: "Our internal budget is frozen / tight"
**Acknowledge:**
"I appreciate that and it happens a lot in planning cycles."

**Reframe:**
"When budgets are constrained, teams usually need the clearest highest-return move. The Sprint can reduce spending by avoiding speculative work."

**Evidence:**
"Scope is fixed and short. We can also phase spending via narrowed scope if that’s the only operational blocker."

**Close attempt:**
"Would a narrower first scope aligned to one priority workflow keep momentum without exceeding your current quarter limits?"

---

## 11) Objection: "No resources to support interviews/access"
**Acknowledge:**
"That constraint is real, and we should design around it."

**Reframe:**
"The Sprint is meant to minimize internal overhead — not add another large operating burden. We can anchor it around pre-existing standups and one consolidated stakeholder block."

**Evidence:**
"Our day-one plan requires only 2–4 key stakeholders and baseline KPI pull; everything else is sequenced from there."

**Close attempt:**
"Which 2–4 people and what 60–90-minute window are realistic for this week? I can work around your load." 

---

## 12) Objection: "We’re not ready for long-term contracts"
**Acknowledge:**
"That makes sense, and we can avoid that with a short-cycle entry."

**Reframe:**
"This first step is a Sprint, not a long commitment. The retainer comes only after you have evidence and leadership-approved priorities."

**Evidence:**
"The Sprint is a one-time diagnostic deliverable with clear deliverables and termination boundary. Retainer is a separate decision stage when expansion signals appear."

**Close attempt:**
"Can we commit just to the Sprint and set retainer discussion to a separate stage based on outcomes?"

---

## 13) Objection: "We already have too many vendors/tools"
**Acknowledge:**
"Tool saturation is a real signal of organizational fatigue."

**Reframe:**
"That’s why this engagement focuses on making your current stack accountable, not replacing existing tooling."

**Evidence:**
"We map to what you already use and rank remediation work based on current capabilities and evidence gaps."

**Close attempt:**
"Would you prefer a 1-page output that identifies what to keep, adjust, or ignore in your current tool mix?"

---

## 14) Objection: "We need to run this by security first"
**Acknowledge:**
"Absolutely, and that’s the right sequence for any serious partner."

**Reframe:**
"Great — the Sprint can actually reduce your security review burden by producing clear evidence and scope boundaries early."

**Evidence:**
"Scoring includes governance and evidence confidence dimensions specifically so security review has concrete checkpoints, not vague claims."

**Close attempt:**
"Shall we include your security point person as a named participant in the post-demo decision checkpoint?"

---

## 15) Objection: "We can’t prioritize this right now"
**Acknowledge:**
"I get it—there are often urgent priorities already in the queue."

**Reframe:**
"Exactly because there are many priorities, this helps you choose correctly: the highest-impact 5-day diagnostic before you spend on broad fixes."

**Evidence:**
"The score + gap ranking tells your team what to defer and what must be fixed first based on risk and business impact."

**Close attempt:**
"If leadership confirms this is not immediate, can we agree a review date with a 1-line decision memo so the item doesn’t stall indefinitely?"

---

## Coaching notes
- Use one objection at a time; do not answer multiple objections at once.
- If the prospect remains resistant after 2 close attempts, move to a next-step decision date and conditional follow-up.
- Always anchor to business outcome and evidence, never abstract capability.
