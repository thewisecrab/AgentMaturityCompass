### SECTION 2 — The AI Agent Readiness Gap  
**Duration: 8 minutes**

**Sub-topic A: What "maturity" actually means (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- Distinguish between model maturity (AI capability) and org maturity (readiness to deploy and sustain AI in production).
- "Most teams are building with capable models. The gap is almost never the model."
- Use a simple 2x2: Model Quality vs. Org Readiness → show that high model quality + low org readiness = failed or stalled launches.
- Key point: Maturity = repeatable, governed, measurable AI deployment capability. Not one impressive demo.

**Sub-topic B: The cost of the readiness gap (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- Describe the pattern: "Pilot → enthusiasm → production blockers → delay → stakeholder frustration → project scope creep → eventual de-prioritization."
- This is not a technology failure — it's a systems and process failure.
- Mention that this cycle is common across teams of different sizes and stacks.
- Compliance note: Do NOT cite specific client outcomes as proof — frame as observed patterns.

**Sub-topic C: The maturity spectrum (2 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- Introduce a simplified maturity spectrum: Stage 1 (Experimenting) → Stage 2 (Piloting) → Stage 3 (Operationalizing) → Stage 4 (Scaling) → Stage 5 (Governing).
- "Most teams we see are stuck between Stage 2 and Stage 3 — the 'pilot purgatory' zone."
- This is the context for the five mistakes we're about to cover.

**Transition language:**  
> "Now — here are the five specific mistakes that keep teams stuck in that purgatory zone. I want you to honestly score yourself as we go through each one."

### SECTION 3 — The 5 Maturity Mistakes  
**Duration: 15 minutes (3 min per mistake)**

> **Presenter tip:** For each mistake, use the same rhythm: Name the mistake → What it looks like → Why it happens → What it costs → What to do instead. Keep it crisp.

---

**Mistake #1 — Confusing a Demo with a Product (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- The mistake: Teams celebrate a working prototype and treat it as production-ready.
- What it looks like: "We showed the CEO and it worked great" — but there's no error handling, no feedback loop, no fallback, no eval framework.
- Why it happens: Pressure to show momentum + AI tools make demos fast and cheap.
- What it costs: Production incidents, trust loss with stakeholders, rollback, delay.
- What to do instead: Define a production readiness checklist *before* the demo. Separate "proof of concept" from "production-ready" in your roadmap language.

**Transition:** > "Mistake #2 is related — and it's about what happens before the demo is even built."

---

**Mistake #2 — Building Without a Measurement Contract (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- The mistake: Launching (or continuing to develop) an AI agent without agreed-upon success metrics.
- What it looks like: "We'll know it's working when stakeholders feel good about it." No eval criteria. No baseline.
- Why it happens: Speed pressure. Metrics feel like a later problem.
- What it costs: Scope creep, stakeholder misalignment, impossible-to-defend budget renewals.
- What to do instead: Before building, define: What does "working" look like? How will we measure it? What's the minimum bar for production? Document it. Get sign-off.

**Transition:** > "Mistake #3 is the one that surprises teams the most — because it's not about AI at all."

---

**Mistake #3 — Skipping the Data Readiness Audit (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- The mistake: Assuming available data = usable data for AI agent workflows.
- What it looks like: Team builds the agent logic, then discovers the data is siloed, inconsistent, or permission-blocked at the integration stage.
- Why it happens: Data is boring. AI is exciting. Audit is a speed bump no one wants to schedule.
- What it costs: 4–12 week delays at integration. Morale damage. Rework.
- What to do instead: Run a lightweight data readiness checklist in week one of any AI agent project: availability, quality, access permissions, update frequency, edge case coverage.

**Transition:** > "Mistake #4 is where governance comes in — and where a lot of technically strong teams have a blind spot."

---

**Mistake #4 — Treating Governance as a Post-Launch Problem (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- The mistake: Deferring responsible AI policies, human-in-the-loop decisions, and escalation protocols until after the agent is built.
- What it looks like: Agent goes to QA. Legal/compliance says: "Wait — who approves what this agent can say or do?" Full stop.
- Why it happens: Governance feels slow and abstract. Engineering moves fast.
- What it costs: Legal review delays (often 3–8 weeks). Scope rollback. Compliance risk exposure.
- What to do instead: Run a lightweight governance sprint in parallel with the build sprint. Define: What can the agent do autonomously? What requires human approval? What are the escalation paths? Document them.

**Transition:** > "And Mistake #5 is the one that kills post-launch momentum — even when the launch itself goes well."

---

**Mistake #5 — No Feedback Loop Architecture (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- The mistake: Launching the agent without a structured system to capture, triage, and act on agent failure signals.
- What it looks like: The agent is live. Things go wrong. No one is sure how bad it is. Fixes are ad hoc. Users lose confidence.
- Why it happens: Teams focus on the "ship it" milestone, not the "sustain it" system.
- What it costs: Compounding quality degradation, stakeholder loss of confidence, eventual shutdown.
- What to do instead: Before launch, define your feedback loop: How will failures surface? Who triages them? How fast? What's the escalation path? Build monitoring into the launch checklist.

**Transition language:**  
> "So — five mistakes. Now the good news: none of these require you to start over. They require a structured way to assess where you are and what to fix first. Let me show you the framework."

### SECTION 4 — The Maturity Roadmap That Works  
**Duration: 7 minutes**

**Sub-topic A: Introducing the Maturity Compass Framework (3 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- Introduce AMC's maturity assessment framework at a conceptual level (not a sales pitch — this is educational).
- Five dimensions: (1) Production Readiness, (2) Measurement & Eval, (3) Data Foundation, (4) Governance & Compliance, (5) Feedback & Iteration Loop.
- "These map directly to the five mistakes we just covered — not by accident."
- Each dimension scored 1–5. Low scores = prioritized fix areas.
- "The point isn't a perfect score. The point is knowing which dimension is your current bottleneck."

**Sub-topic B: What a good next 30 days looks like (2 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- Give a concrete, practical 30-day outline for a team stuck at Stage 2→3:
  - Week 1: Audit + score the five dimensions (a half-day exercise)
  - Week 2: Prioritize the top 2 gaps; design targeted fixes
  - Week 3: Prototype the fix with stakeholders in the loop
  - Week 4: Re-score + update roadmap with evidence
- "This is the pattern that moves teams from pilot purgatory to production confidence."

**Sub-topic C: What to do if you're not sure where you are (2 min)**  
Speaker notes:
- "If you're listening to this and you're not sure which of the five mistakes is your bottleneck right now — that's exactly the starting point."
- Introduce the 15-min Roadmap Review: a quick structured conversation to identify the primary bottleneck and give a prioritized next-step recommendation.
- "We'll open a limited number of spots after today's session — more on that in a moment."

**Transition language:**  
> "Before we get there, let's open up for questions. What's coming up for you? What did we miss? What's the mistake that stings the most for your team?"
